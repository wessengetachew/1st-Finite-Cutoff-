The Unifying Identity
(p-1)(p-2)/p² = [1 - 1/(p-1)²] × [1 - 1/p]³

A simple modular arithmetic expression reveals the hidden algebraic connection between three foundational results in number theory.

1
Euler's Zeta Function Products
1737

Leonhard Euler discovered the fundamental connection between prime numbers and the Riemann zeta function through his famous product formula:

ζ(s) = ∏(p prime) 1/(1 - p^(-s)) = ∏(p prime) 1/(1 - 1/p^s)

The factors [1 - 1/p] that appear in our identity are directly related to Euler's original work. When s = 1, the zeta function diverges, but these individual factors encode fundamental information about prime distribution.

Euler's insight was revolutionary: he connected the discrete world of prime numbers to the continuous world of analysis, laying the foundation for all modern analytic number theory.

Connection to our identity: The factor [1 - 1/p]³ is the cube of Euler's fundamental building block.

2
Mertens' Prime Density Theorem
1874

Franz Mertens proved a remarkable result about the density of primes, establishing that despite the infinite product diverging, a logarithmic scaling reveals a beautiful constant:

lim(n→∞) ln(n) × ∏(p≤n) [1 - 1/p] = e^(-γ)

where γ ≈ 0.5772156 is the Euler-Mascheroni constant.

This theorem showed that the "sieving" effect of primes—removing multiples from the integers—follows a precise logarithmic law. The factors [1 - 1/p] represent the fraction of integers that survive each prime's sieve.

Mertens' work was groundbreaking because it provided exact constants for prime distribution without requiring the sophisticated complex analysis that later proved the Prime Number Theorem.

Connection to our identity: Our factor [1 - 1/p]³ is exactly the cube of Mertens' fundamental factor.

3
Hardy-Littlewood Twin Prime Constants
1923

G.H. Hardy and J.E. Littlewood conjectured a precise asymptotic formula for twin primes, introducing the famous twin prime constant:

C₂ = ∏(p≥3) [1 - 1/(p-1)²] ≈ 0.6601618

This constant appears in their conjectured asymptotic formula for the number of twin primes up to x:

π₂(x) ~ 2C₂ × x/(ln x)²

The factor [1 - 1/(p-1)²] accounts for the "collision probability" in twin prime formation—the chance that both n and n+2 avoid being divisible by prime p.

Though still unproven, this conjecture has guided twin prime research for over a century and connects to the deepest problems in number theory.

Connection to our identity: The factor [1 - 1/(p-1)²] appears exactly as Hardy and Littlewood defined it.

Extended Results: Telescoping and Finite Products
2025

The primary identity leads to two additional remarkable results that further demonstrate the deep structure underlying these expressions.

Telescoping Product Identity

When we extend the identity to all integers (not just primes), we discover a beautiful telescoping product:

∏(k=3 to n) [(k-1)(k-2)/k²] = 4/[n²(n-1)]

This identity reveals that the infinite product of our modular expressions telescopes to a simple rational function. The proof elegantly separates into two independent telescoping products:

First Factor:
∏(k=3 to n) [(k-1)/k] = 2/n
Second Factor:
∏(k=3 to n) [(k-2)/k] = 2/[n(n-1)]

Multiplying these telescoping products gives our final result: (2/n) × (2/[n(n-1)]) = 4/[n²(n-1)].

Finite Prime Products

For products taken over finite sets of primes, our main identity factorizes beautifully:

∏(3≤p≤p_max) [(p-1)(p-2)/p²] = C₂(p_max) × [M(p_max)]³

Where:

  • C₂(p_max) = ∏(3≤p≤p_max) [1 - 1/(p-1)²] - Finite Hardy-Littlewood approximation
  • M(p_max) = ∏(3≤p≤p_max) [1 - 1/p] - Finite Mertens factor

As p_max → ∞, we have C₂(p_max) → C₂ ≈ 0.6601618 and M(p_max) → 0, but ln(p_max) × M(p_max) → e^(-γ) by Mertens' theorem.

The Complete Story

What began as a simple question about modular arithmetic has revealed a remarkable mathematical structure. The expression (p-1)(p-2)/p² is not just another formula—it's a bridge connecting three centuries of number theory development.

From Euler's foundational work on infinite products, through Mertens' precise constants, to Hardy-Littlewood's twin prime conjectures, these discoveries are revealed to be facets of a single underlying algebraic truth. The telescoping identities and finite product formulas further demonstrate that this connection extends beyond individual results to encompass entire mathematical frameworks.

This reminds us that mathematics is deeply interconnected, and that the most profound insights often come from recognizing that seemingly separate discoveries are actually different expressions of the same fundamental structure.
